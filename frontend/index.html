Musikalisches Würfelspiel (K. 516f)

Mozart composed 176 musical measures. By rolling two dice for each of 16 bars, you select measures from a table to compose a unique minuet. There are 1116 ≈ 4.6 × 1016 possible compositions.

The Mathematics: Two dice produce sums 2–12, but not uniformly. Sum 7 has probability 6/36 = 1/6 (most likely), while 2 and 12 each have probability 1/36. Mozart weighted his most interesting measures toward the center of the table, where dice land most often.

Harmonic Series Explorer

Every musical note is actually a sum of sine waves at integer multiples of a fundamental frequency. Click harmonics to hear and visualize them.

The Mathematics: The nth harmonic has frequency n×f. The octave is 2f (ratio 2:1), the perfect fifth is 3f/2 (ratio 3:2), the major third is 5f/4 (ratio 5:4). These simple integer ratios are why certain intervals sound consonant — the waveforms align periodically.

Euclidean Rhythm Generator

Distribute k beats as evenly as possible across n steps using Euclid's algorithm (the same one for GCD). This produces rhythms found in African, Cuban, and Middle Eastern music.

The Mathematics: E(k,n) distributes k onsets among n slots using Bjorklund's algorithm (equivalent to Bresenham's line algorithm and Euclid's GCD algorithm). E(3,8) = [x . . x . . x .] is the Cuban tresillo. E(5,8) is the Cuban cinquillo. E(7,12) produces a pattern found in West African bell patterns.

Fibonacci in Musical Scales

The piano keyboard encodes Fibonacci numbers: 1 octave, 2 types of keys (black/white), 3 black keys in a group, 5 black keys total, 8 white keys, 13 keys total per octave.

The Mathematics: The Fibonacci sequence 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13... appears naturally in the chromatic scale. The pentatonic scale (5 notes) and diatonic scale (8 notes within 13 chromatic steps) arise from the same pattern. The golden ratio φ = 1.618... governs where climax points feel most natural in a composition — typically at 61.8% of the way through.

Pythagorean Tuning vs Equal Temperament

Pythagoras discovered that pleasing intervals come from simple ratios (3:2 for a fifth, 4:3 for a fourth). But stacking twelve perfect fifths doesn't return to the same note — the gap is the Pythagorean comma.

The Mathematics: A pure fifth is ×3/2. Stacking 12 fifths: (3/2)12 = 129.746... but 7 octaves = 27 = 128. The ratio 129.746/128 ≈ 1.01364 is the Pythagorean comma (~23.46 cents). Equal temperament compromises: each semitone = 21/12 ≈ 1.05946, so a tempered fifth = 27/12 ≈ 1.4983 instead of exactly 1.5.

Fast Fourier Transform Lab

Decompose signals into frequencies, edit them, and reconstruct. Works for both audio and images.

1 Record or Load Audio
Sound is invisible wiggles in the air. When you talk, your throat vibrates and pushes air in a wavy pattern. A microphone catches these wiggles and turns them into numbers — one number for each tiny moment in time. That list of numbers is your "signal."
A microphone samples the air pressure thousands of times per second (typically 44,100 Hz). Each sample is a number between -1 and 1. This sequence x[0], x[1], ..., x[N-1] is the discrete time-domain signal. The sampling rate must be at least 2× the highest frequency you want to capture (Nyquist theorem).
The continuous signal x(t) is sampled at rate fs to produce x[n] = x(n/fs). By the Nyquist-Shannon theorem, if x(t) is band-limited to B Hz, then fs ≥ 2B suffices for perfect reconstruction: x(t) = ∑ x[n] · sinc(fst - n). Quantization maps each sample to a finite bit-depth (16-bit = 65,536 levels).
Digital audio is a sampled, quantized representation. At 44.1 kHz / 16-bit, each second produces 88.2 KB of raw PCM data. The sampling theorem guarantees lossless reconstruction for signals below fs/2 = 22.05 kHz — conveniently above human hearing (~20 kHz). In practice, an anti-aliasing filter is applied before sampling to prevent spectral folding.
2 Decompose — Forward FFT
Imagine your voice is a smoothie made of different fruits. The FFT is like a magic un-blender — it takes the smoothie apart and tells you exactly how much of each fruit (frequency) is inside! Low sounds are like strawberries, high sounds are like blueberries. The tall bars in the chart show which "fruits" are loudest.
The FFT converts your signal from time domain (amplitude vs. time) to frequency domain (amplitude vs. frequency). Every sound is a mix of pure sine waves at different frequencies. The FFT figures out which frequencies are present and how strong each one is. The formula: X[k] = ∑ x[n] · e-2πi·kn/N. Brute force takes N² operations; the FFT shortcut does it in N·log(N).
DFT: X[k] = ∑n=0N-1 x[n] · e-j2πkn/N, k = 0, ..., N-1.
Cooley-Tukey FFT: Split into even/odd indices: X[k] = E[k] + WkN · O[k], where E and O are N/2-point DFTs, WN = e-j2π/N. Recursion depth = log2N, each level does N multiplies ⇒ O(N log N).
Proof of correctness: The N-th roots of unity {WNk} form an orthogonal basis for ℂN. The DFT is the change-of-basis matrix. Inverse exists because the Vandermonde matrix of roots of unity is non-singular.
The DFT is a linear map F: ℂN → ℂN represented by the matrix Fkn = ωkn, ω = e-2πi/N. Cooley-Tukey exploits the factorization FN = (IN/2 ⊗ F2) · T · (FN/2 ⊕ FN/2) · P, where T contains twiddle factors and P is the bit-reversal permutation. This reduces multiply count from N² to (N/2)log2N. In signal processing pipelines, overlap-add or overlap-save methods apply FFT-based convolution for real-time filtering at O(N log N) per frame.
3 Edit — Remove Frequency Bands
Now you get to play DJ! Each slider controls a group of frequencies. Slide one down to zero and that sound disappears — like muting the drums but keeping the singing. You're choosing which "ingredients" stay in the smoothie!
Each frequency bin X[k] has a magnitude (loudness) and phase (timing). By setting a bin's magnitude to zero, you remove that frequency from the signal. This is frequency-domain filtering. Removing low frequencies = high-pass filter (removes bass). Removing high frequencies = low-pass filter (removes hiss). This is exactly how noise cancellation works!
Filtering in the frequency domain is multiplication: Y[k] = H[k] · X[k], where H[k] is the transfer function. By the convolution theorem, this equals circular convolution in the time domain: y[n] = (h * x)[n]. An ideal brick-wall filter (H[k]=0 for |k|>kc) has a sinc impulse response, which is non-causal and infinite-length — hence real filters use windowed approximations (Hamming, Hann, Blackman).
Spectral editing multiplies the DFT by a diagonal matrix H = diag(H[0], ..., H[N-1]). The convolution theorem states F{h*x} = F{h} · F{x}. Zeroing bins is an ideal (but discontinuous) filter; in practice, smooth roll-off windows reduce spectral leakage and Gibbs ringing. For audio, overlap-add with Hann windows (75% overlap) provides artifact-free real-time processing. The modified spectrum maintains Hermitian symmetry for real-valued output: X[N-k] = X[k]*.
4 Reconstruct — Inverse FFT & Play
Now the magic un-blender runs backwards — it re-blends the fruits you kept into a new smoothie! The sound you hear is the rebuilt version, with the parts you removed now gone. It's like erasing certain colors from a painting and seeing what's left!
The Inverse FFT reverses the process: x[n] = (1/N) ∑ X[k] · e2πi·kn/N. Notice it's almost the same formula as the forward FFT, just with a positive exponent and a 1/N scaling factor. The FFT and IFFT are mathematical inverses — apply both and you get back exactly where you started (if you didn't edit anything).
IDFT: x[n] = (1/N) ∑k=0N-1 X[k] · ej2πkn/N.
Proof that F-1F = I: (F-1F)mn = (1/N) ∑k ω-mkωkn = (1/N) ∑k ωk(n-m). For n=m, each term is 1, sum = N, result = 1. For n≠m, it's a geometric series summing to (ωN(n-m)-1)/(ω(n-m)-1) = 0, since ωN=1. QED.
The IFFT uses the same Cooley-Tukey structure with conjugate twiddle factors.
The DFT matrix F is unitary up to scaling: F*F = NI, so F-1 = (1/N)F*. Implementation trick: IFFT(X) = (1/N) · conj(FFT(conj(X))), so a single FFT routine handles both directions. Parseval's theorem confirms energy conservation: ∑|x[n]|² = (1/N)∑|X[k]|². After spectral editing, comparing the energy of original vs. modified signals quantifies exactly how much signal power was removed by the filter.
1 Load Image
Original
Grayscale
A picture is made of tiny dots called pixels. Each pixel has a brightness number — 0 is black, 255 is white. The whole image is just a big grid of numbers! We convert it to gray first so we only need one number per pixel instead of three (red, green, blue).
A digital image is a 2D array (matrix) of pixel values. Grayscale conversion uses the luminance formula: Y = 0.299R + 0.587G + 0.114B. A 256×256 image has 65,536 pixel values. This matrix is the "spatial domain" — each cell says how bright that location is.
The image f(x,y) ∈ ℝM×N is a real-valued matrix. Grayscale conversion applies the ITU-R BT.601 luma: Y = 0.299R + 0.587G + 0.114B. For 2D DFT, we work with a square power-of-2 matrix (resize to 256×256). Each entry f(x,y) represents spatial-domain intensity.
In image processing pipelines, the input is typically converted to single-channel float32 and resized to a power-of-2 dimension for FFT efficiency. The BT.601 luma weights (0.299, 0.587, 0.114) model human eye sensitivity. For color images, FFT can be applied per-channel, but grayscale simplifies the demonstration of frequency-domain concepts.
2 2D FFT — Decompose into Spatial Frequencies
Magnitude Spectrum (log)
Phase
The 2D FFT does the same magic un-blending — but for a picture instead of sound! The bright dot in the center of the spectrum means "smooth, even areas." Dots far from the center mean "sharp edges and fine details." The picture has been taken apart into its pattern ingredients!
The 2D FFT breaks the image into spatial frequencies — how fast brightness changes across the image. Low frequencies (center of spectrum) = gradual gradients and overall brightness. High frequencies (edges of spectrum) = sharp edges and fine textures. The formula applies 1D FFT to each row, then each column. Magnitude tells you how strong each pattern is; phase tells you where it's positioned.
2D DFT: F(u,v) = ∑x=0M-1 ∑y=0N-1 f(x,y) · e-j2π(ux/M + vy/N).
This is separable: apply 1D DFT along rows, then along columns. Complexity: O(MN·log(MN)). The spectrum is shifted so DC (F(0,0) = ∑f(x,y), the average brightness) is at the center. We display log(1+|F(u,v)|) because the dynamic range spans several orders of magnitude.
The 2D DFT is separable, enabling row-column decomposition: apply N 1D FFTs along rows (O(MN log N)), then M 1D FFTs along columns (O(MN log M)). Total: O(MN log(MN)). The magnitude spectrum |F(u,v)| is symmetric (Hermitian for real input). The DC component F(0,0) equals the sum of all pixel values. Phase encodes spatial structure — swapping the phase of two images swaps their perceived content, proving that phase carries more perceptual information than magnitude.
3 Edit — Frequency Domain Filtering
Filter Mask
Filtered Spectrum
Now you're an image DJ! Low-pass keeps only the smooth parts — the picture gets blurry like looking through foggy glass. High-pass keeps only the edges — you see the outlines like a coloring book! You're choosing which "patterns" to keep in your picture.
The filter mask is a circle in the frequency domain. Low-pass (keep center) = blur, removes detail. High-pass (keep edges) = edge detection, removes smooth areas. Band-pass keeps only a ring of frequencies. This is literally how Instagram blur effects and Photoshop sharpening work under the hood — they're multiplying the spectrum by a mask!
Filtering: G(u,v) = H(u,v) · F(u,v), where H is the transfer function. An ideal low-pass filter: H(u,v) = 1 if √(u²+v²) ≤ D0, else 0. This causes ringing (Gibbs phenomenon) due to the sharp cutoff. Butterworth and Gaussian filters provide smoother roll-off: HGauss(u,v) = e-D(u,v)²/(2D0²). The convolution theorem guarantees this equals spatial-domain convolution with the impulse response h(x,y) = F-1{H}.
Frequency-domain filtering is the backbone of image processing: denoising (Wiener filter), sharpening (unsharp mask = original + α·high-pass), and compression (JPEG uses DCT, a real-valued cousin of DFT). MRI reconstruction applies inverse 2D FFT to k-space data acquired by the scanner — the raw MRI signal IS the Fourier transform of the body slice. CT reconstruction uses the Fourier Slice Theorem: 1D FT of a projection at angle θ equals a slice through the 2D FT at that angle.
4 Reconstruct — Inverse 2D FFT
Original
Reconstructed
The inverse FFT puts the picture back together from whatever patterns you kept! If you kept only the smooth parts, the picture looks blurry. If you kept only the edges, you see an outline drawing. You've just done what Photoshop does — but you understand the MATH behind it!
The 2D Inverse FFT: f(x,y) = (1/MN) ∑∑ F(u,v) · ej2π(ux/M + vy/N). It reconstructs each pixel by adding up all the frequency components you kept. Compare original vs. reconstructed side by side — you can literally SEE which information lives in which frequencies!
2D IDFT: f(x,y) = (1/MN) ∑u=0M-1 ∑v=0N-1 F(u,v) · ej2π(ux/M+vy/N).
Separable: apply 1D IFFT along columns, then rows. The result is real-valued (imaginary parts are numerical noise < 10-10) because the input was a real image with Hermitian spectrum. Parseval: ∑|f(x,y)|² = (1/MN)∑|F(u,v)|² — energy in the filtered image equals energy of the retained spectral components.
The reconstruction demonstrates the perfect invertibility of the DFT. In practice, JPEG compression applies a block-wise DCT (8×8), quantizes high-frequency coefficients to zero, and inverse-transforms to get the compressed image. The quality slider literally controls how many frequency components are discarded. This lab replicates that pipeline at full-image scale, making the compression-quality tradeoff viscerally visible.
